## 01. Information Systems Overview

### 1.1 IT vs IS

- **Information Technology (IT)**: all the hardware and software that a firm needs to use in order to achieve its business objectives. IT generally consists of:
  - Computing technology
  - Networking (communication) technology
  - Data management and analytics technology
- **Information System (IS)**: a set of interrelated components that collect, process, store, and distribute information to support decision making, coordination, and control. Help managers and workers analyze problems, visualize complex subjects, and create new products.
  - Example: checkout system in a supermarket — hardware, software, and people dealing with information about products, customers, sales, and payments.
- **Data vs Information**:
  - Data are streams of raw facts.
  - Information is data shaped into meaningful form. Alternative: information is the meaning conveyed in data.

### 1.2 Functions of an Information System

Three basic activities: **Input → Processing → Output**. Feedback is output returned to appropriate people or activities in the organization to evaluate and refine the input. Environmental actors (customers, suppliers, competitors, stockholders, regulatory agencies) interact with the organization and its IS.

### 1.3 What is MIS?

- **Narrow sense (traditional)**: IS that support the middle-level management of an organization.
- **Broad sense (general)**: systems built with IT that facilitate management excellence (MIS = IS). Also refers to the disciplinary field that studies the construction, use, and impacts of information systems.

### 1.4 Three Dimensions of Information Systems

Using IS effectively requires an understanding of organization, management, and IT shaping the systems. An IS creates value as an organizational and management solution to challenges posed by the environment.

**(1) Organization**: people, structure, business processes, politics, and culture.
- Hierarchy: Senior management → Middle management → Operational management → Knowledge workers → Data workers → Production or service workers
- Separation of business functions: Sales and marketing, Human resources, Finance and accounting, Manufacturing and production
- Unique business processes, unique business culture, organizational politics

**(2) Management**: managers set organizational strategy for responding to business challenges; must act creatively — create new products/services, occasionally re-create the organization.
- Organization = structure of the firm; Management = decisions and moves taken by managers

**(3) Technology**: computer hardware and software, data management and analytics technology, networking and telecommunications technology. IT infrastructure provides the platform that the system is built on.

### 1.11 Organization: Technical vs Behavioral Views

- **Technical (microeconomic) definition**: formal social structure that processes resources from environment to produce outputs. Inputs (capital, labor) → Production process → Outputs. When technology changes, the technical view focuses on how inputs are combined to create outputs → building IS is to re-shape the production process. However, this is incomplete.
- **Behavioral definition**: a collection of rights, privileges, obligations, and responsibilities that is delicately balanced over time through conflict and conflict resolution. Building new IS involves much more than technical rearrangement — it changes the organizational balance, can take a long time, be very disruptive, and requires more resources for training and learning.

### 1.20 Considerations When Building IS

- **Goals**: Is the system aligned with the goals of the firm?
- **Structure**: Does the system fit with the organizational structure? Is it possible/beneficial to change the structure?

### 1.22 Types of IS — Details

**Transaction Processing Systems (TPS)**
- Serve operational managers and staff
- Perform and record daily routine transactions (e.g., sales order entry, payroll, shipping)
- Allow managers to monitor status of operations and relations with external environment
- Serve predefined, structured goals and decision making

**Management Information Systems (MIS)**
- Serve middle management
- Provide reports on firm's current performance, based on data from TPS
- Provide answers to routine questions with predefined procedures
- Typically have little analytic capability

**Decision Support Systems (DSS)**
- Serve middle management
- Support nonroutine decision making (e.g., "What is the impact on production schedule if December sales doubled?")
- May use external information as well as TPS/MIS data

**Executive Support Systems (ESS)**
- Support senior management; address nonroutine decisions requiring judgment, evaluation, and insight
- Incorporate data about external events (e.g., new tax laws or competitors) as well as summarized internal information
- Example: Digital dashboard with real-time view of firm's financial performance

### 2.2 Technology Drivers of Infrastructure Evolution

- **Moore's Law**: Computing power doubles every 2 years
- **Law of Mass Digital Storage**: Amount of data being stored each year doubles
- **Metcalfe's Law**: Value/power of a network grows exponentially as a function of the number of network members
- **Declining communication costs and the Internet**: Exponential growth in size of the Internet
- **Standards and network effects**: Specifications that establish the compatibility of products

### 2.6 Database Management Systems (DBMS)

- **Database**: a collection of data organized to serve many applications efficiently by centralizing data and controlling redundant data
- **DBMS**: software that permits an organization to centralize data, manage them efficiently, and provide access to stored data by application programs
- Represents data as two-dimensional tables
- **Rows (tuples)**: Records for different entities
- **Fields (columns)**: Represents attribute for entity
- **Key field**: Field used to uniquely identify each record
- **Primary key**: Field in table used for key fields
- **Foreign key**: Primary key used in second table as look-up field to identify records from original table

### 2.7 Emerging Database Forms

**Non-relational databases: NoSQL**
- More flexible data model; data sets stored across distributed machines; easier to scale; handle large volumes of unstructured and structured data

**Vector databases (向量数据库)**
- Stores unstructured data (text, images, audio) as high-dimensional numerical arrays (embeddings) generated by neural networks
- Executes semantic similarity search to find data with similar meaning
- Functions as critical "long-term memory" infrastructure for Generative AI, powering Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architectures

**Databases in the cloud**
- Examples: Aliyun RDS, Amazon AWS Database, Microsoft SQL Azure Database
- Benefits: better respond to real-time demand, keep costs low

### 2.8 Databases and the Web

- Many companies use the web to make internal databases available to customers or partners
- Typical configuration: Multi-tier Server — Web server → Application server/middleware/CGI scripts → Database server (hosting DBMS)
- Advantages: ease of use of browser software; web interface requires few or no changes to database; inexpensive to add web interface

### 2.9 Data Warehouse

- Stores current and historical data from many core operational transaction systems
- Consolidates and standardizes information for use across enterprise, but data cannot be altered
- Provides analysis and reporting tools
- Characteristics: Subject oriented, Multiple dimensions, Information summaries and aggregations

### 2.10 Analytical Tools

**Online Analytical Processing (OLAP)**
- Supports multidimensional data analysis; each aspect (product, pricing, cost, region, time period) is different dimension
- Operations: Data Cube, Slice (切片), Dice (切块), Pivot (旋转), Drill down (下钻), Roll up (上卷)
- Enables rapid, online answers to ad hoc queries

**Data Mining**
- Finds hidden patterns, relationships in datasets
- Types of information obtainable:
  - **Associations (关联)**: e.g., beer → diaper; Technique: Apriori Algorithm (基于"置信度"和"支持度")
  - **Sequences (序列)**: e.g., stock price prediction, image/audio recognition; Techniques: time-series regression, temporal association rules, Markov chains, Neural Networks/Deep Learning
  - **Classification (分类)**: e.g., credit evaluation; Techniques: decision trees, neural networks, support vector machines, random forests
  - **Clustering (聚类)**: e.g., customer segmentation by income/debt; Techniques: K-means, density-based clustering, hierarchical clustering
  - **Forecasting (预测)**

**Text Mining and Web Mining**
- Text mining: extracts key elements from large unstructured data sets; sentiment analysis
- Web mining: discovery and analysis of useful patterns from web (content mining, structure mining, usage mining)

### 2.11 Large Language Models (LLM) in Business Contexts

Four approaches, from shallow to deep integration:
1. **"Connect to" general LLM services** — Key: **prompt engineering** (提示词工程)
2. **Customized LLM** — customize in business context — Key: **fine-tuning** (微调)

### 2.15 Security Threats

**Malicious Software (Malware, 恶意软件)**:
- Virus (病毒): attaches to other programs/data files, executed without user knowledge
- Worms (蠕虫): self-copying programs spread over networks
- Trojan horse (特洛伊木马): appears benign but acts maliciously
- Ransomware (勒索软件): extorts money by taking control of computers
- Spyware (间谍软件): monitors user web-surfing activities
- Keyloggers (按键窃听器): record every keystroke
- Code injection (代码注入): e.g., SQL injection, prompt injection

**Hackers and Computer Crime**:
- System intrusion (系统侵入): accessing without authority
- Spoofing (欺诈): redirecting to wrong address
- Sniffing (嗅探): monitors network traffic
- Denial-of-Service (DoS/DDoS, 拒绝服务攻击)
- Identity theft (身份窃取)
- Click Fraud (点击欺骗)

**Internal Threats**: Employees
**Software Vulnerability**
**Natural Disasters**

### 2.16 Security Tools and Technologies

- **Identity Management (身份管理) and Authentication (认证)**: Passwords, Tokens, Smart cards, Biometric authentication, Two-factor authentication
- **Firewalls (防火墙)**: combination of hardware and software preventing unauthorized access
- **Intrusion Detection Systems (入侵检测系统)**
- **Antivirus Software (杀毒软件)**
- **Encryption (加密)**:
  - Symmetric key encryption: sender and receiver use single shared key
  - Asymmetric key (public key) encryption: uses two mathematically related keys (public + private); sender encrypts with recipient's public key; recipient decrypts with private key
  - Typical algorithm: RSA; Typical tool: OpenPGP
- **Digital certificate (数字证书)**: data file to establish identity for online transactions
- **Certification authority (CA, 认证机构)**: trusted third party verifying identity
- **Public key infrastructure (PKI)**: public key cryptography working with CA; widely used in e-commerce
- **High-availability Systems**: Redundancy (冗余) — no "single-failure point" (无"单一失败点"); Backup (备份) — recover from collapse

## 03. Enterprise Applications

### 3.1 Enterprise Applications Overview

Systems for linking the enterprise — span functional areas, execute business processes across the firm, include all levels of management. Four major applications: ERP, SCM, CRM, KMS.

### 3.2 Evolution of Manufacturing System Management

**ROP (Reorder Point, 订货点法)**: Early stage inventory management (1940s)

**MRP (Material Requirement Planning)**:
- Production planning, scheduling, and inventory control system to manage manufacturing processes
- Three objectives: (1) Material — Ensure materials available for production; (2) Requirement — Maintain lowest possible material/product levels; (3) Plan — Plan manufacturing, delivery, purchasing activities
- Uses Bill of Materials (BOM): comprehensive list of parts, assemblies, materials to create a product
- Theoretic foundation: Operations Research (OR, 运筹学)
- **Limitations**: Focusing only on materials; assuming no constraints on production capacity/procurement/manpower; no cost info integrated; lack of feedback capability; poor data integrity

**MRPII (Manufacturing Resource Planning)**:
- Method for effective planning of all resources directly related to production
- Resources: Money, Manpower, Materials, production capacity, etc.
- Ideally addresses operational planning, financial planning, and has simulation capability ("what-if")
- **Limitations**: Ultimate goal is maximizing value (profit) — optimizing production is just one key process; only focusing on production was almost correct in Shortage Economy context but inadequate in Abundance Economy context

**ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning)**:
- Business management software/system — suite of integrated applications
- Collect, store, manage, and interpret data from many business activities: Product planning/cost, Manufacturing/service delivery, Marketing and sales, Inventory management, Shipping and payment

### 3.3 ERP Characteristics

- **Integration**: functional areas are integrated
- **Centralization**: databases are centralized
- Suite of integrated software modules + common central database
- Information entered in one process is immediately available for other processes
- Built around thousands of predefined business processes reflecting **best practices** (最佳实践)

### 3.4 Implementing ERP

To implement, firms:
1. Select functions of system they wish to use
2. Conduct **business process re-engineering** (业务流程重组)
3. Map business processes to software processes
4. Use software's configuration tables for customizing

**Challenges**:
- Conflicts with business strategy — Are "best practices" really "best" for your business?
- Huge investments — Losing cost advantages?
- Conflicts with organizational culture and politics — Organizational resistance (组织抵制)
- Changes in employee responsibilities — Can employees obtain required skills? Will they behave as expected?
- Loss of flexibility — Can the system be adjusted when contextual factors change?

### 3.5 Key Success Factors for Implementing ERP

- The organization is prepared for the change
- The initiative is seen as a business imperative
- Executive leaders are active and visible in support
- Resources are available to conduct the project completely
- A good packaged system is used and customized
- Users are trained before implementation
- Stress the enterprise, not the system
- General manager acts as mediator between imperatives of technology and business

### 3.7 Supply Chain Management (SCM)

**Supply Chain**: Network of organizations and processes for procuring materials, transforming them into products, and distributing the products
- **Upstream**: firm's suppliers, suppliers' suppliers, processes for managing relationships
- **Downstream**: organizations and processes for delivering products to customers
- **Internal**: within the firm

**Supply Chain Efficiency**:
- Inefficiencies can waste up to 25% of operating expenses
- US spent $1.64 trillion on logistics in 2018 (8.0% of GDP)

**Just-in-time (准时制) strategy**: Components arrive as needed; finished goods shipped after assembly line
- Requires perfect information about exactly how many units customers want, when, and when they can be produced

**Reality — Uncertainties**: uncertain product demand, late shipments, defective parts, production breakdowns
- **Safety stock**: buffer for lack of flexibility
- **Bullwhip effect (牛鞭效应)**: information about product demand gets distorted as it passes from one entity to next across supply chain

### 3.8 Causes of the Bullwhip Effect

Fundamental problems: Delay, No collaboration, No information sharing, Poor forecast

Specific causes:
- Customers' orders are uncertain and oscillated
- Replenishment is usually delayed (long lead time)
- Order batching due to economies of scale
- Price fluctuations driven by promotions
- Rationing and shortage gaming (incentive to over-order during shortages, cancel when inventory arrives)

Mechanism: Each company satisfies customer orders by inventory and sends estimated (magnified) orders to suppliers → suppliers further magnify → when real demand declines, decline is also magnified.

### 3.9 Case: Wal-Mart & P&G — VMI (Vendor Managed Inventory)

- **背景**: 1980年，沃尔玛圣路易斯店请求宝洁对帮宝适实施自动连续补货。
- **做法**: 宝洁经理设计连接了沃尔玛库存数据库与宝洁生产仓库数据库——当货架几乎空时，自动从供应商发货，每月结算付款。1987年扩展至全线产品。
- **结果**: 沃尔玛零售业约50%成本为库存持有成本，宝洁为沃尔玛节省了20%库存持有成本。宝洁获得实时数据，每天精确配送匹配预测销量，库存和缺货最小化。
- **核心启示**: 协作(Collaboration)能显著规避风险/不确定性从而降低成本——Vendor Managed Inventory (VMI, 供应商管理库存)。

### 3.10 Push Strategy vs Pull Strategy

| | Push | Pull |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Trigger | Downstream demand forecasts | Actual demand / actual status |
| Posture | "Sell what we make" (efficiency) | "Make what we sell" (responsiveness) |
| Problem | Forecast uncertainty | Delayed information |

**Integrated "Push-Pull" Strategy (Spiral-up)**:
- Hybrid to overcome disadvantages of both
- Early stages: "push" manner — partial production based on aggregated demand forecasts (more accurate, uncertainty reduced)
- Final production: "pull" manner — based on actual customer demand for specific configurations

**Trade-off**: Functional products (stable demand, low margin) vs. Innovative products (unpredictable demand, high margin) → Decoupling Points (DP)

### 3.12 Customer Relationship Management (CRM)

Three functional areas:
- **Marketing (营销)**: getting people interested in your product/service
- **Sales (销售)**: activities leading to selling of goods/services
- **Service (客户服务)**: support for easy and enjoyable customer experience

CRM is an integrated system for:
- Identifying, acquiring, and maintaining customers
- Supporting marketing, sales, and service
- Capturing and integrating customer data from all over the organization
- Consolidating and analyzing customer data
- Distributing customer information to various systems and touch points
- Providing single enterprise view of customers; consistent messaging

Typical modules:
- **Sales force automation (SFA)**: sales prospect/lead and contact info; quote generation
- **Customer service**: assigning/managing service requests; web-based self-service
- **Marketing**: capturing prospect/customer data; cross-selling (交叉销售), up-selling (升级销售), down-selling (降级销售)

### 3.13 Three Types of CRM

| Type | Focus | Key Features |
|:---|:---|:---|

## 04. e-Commerce, Knowledge Management, and Decision Making

### 4.1 Why e-Commerce is Different — Eight Unique Features

1. **Ubiquity**: Marketspace is virtual; transaction costs reduced
2. **Global reach**: Transactions cross cultural and national boundaries
3. **Universal standards**: One set of technology standards (Internet standards)

### 4.2 Digital Markets Are Different

**Two-sided Markets (双边市场)**: Also called two-sided/multi-sided networks/platforms. Technology enables new forms of connecting different users to create value.

### 4.3 Digital Goods Are Different

Three categories of digital goods:
1. **Information products**: e-books, news, photos, music, video
2. **Symbols and data records**: Tickets, financial instruments (checks, e-money, credit cards, securities)
3. **Services**: Messaging (telephone, SMS, email, IM); Business transactions (advertisement, orders, contracts); Entertainment, social networking

- Physical: Non-depletion (无损耗性), Variable (可变性), Replicable (可复制性)

### 4.4 New Possibilities from Digital Goods

- **Bundling (捆绑销售)**: complementarity, competition for markets, reshaping demand curve
- **New forms of value creation**: Two-sided markets

### 4.5 The Long Tail

### 4.6 From Bundling to Subscription (订阅)

- Maximizing CLTV: subscriptions lower entry barrier, extract greater total value over time

## 05. Building and Managing Information Systems

### 5.1 Systems Development and Organizational Change

Four degrees of organizational change (from low to high risk/return):

| Level | Description |
|:---|:---|
| **Automation (自动化)** | Increases efficiency; replaces manual tasks |
| **Rationalization (合理化)** | Streamlines standard operating procedures; continuous quality improvement (TQM, Six Sigma) |
| **Business Process Redesign (业务流程重组, BPR)** | Analyze, simplify, and redesign business processes; reorganize workflow, combine steps, eliminate repetition |
| **Paradigm Shift (模式转换)** | Rethink nature of business; define new business model; change nature of organization |

### 5.2 Business Process Re-engineering (BPR)

- Also called: Business Process Re-engineering (BPR)
- Business Process Management (BPM): variety of tools, methodologies to analyze, design, optimize processes
- Steps: Identify processes for change → Analyze existing processes → Design the new process → Implement the new process → Continuous measurement

### 5.3 Core Activities in the Systems Development Process

| Core Activity | Description |
|:---|:---|
| **Systems analysis** | Identify problem(s); Specify solutions; Establish information requirements |
| **Systems design** | Create design specifications |
| **Programming** | Translate design specifications into program code |
| **Testing** | Unit testing; Systems testing; Acceptance testing |
| **Conversion** | Plan conversion; Prepare documentation; Train users and technical staff |
| **Production and maintenance** | Operate the system; Evaluate the system; Modify the system |

### 5.4 Systems Analysis

- Analysis of problem to be solved by new system: Defining the problem, Identifying causes, Process modeling (as-is)
- Specifying solutions: Identifying information requirements, Process modeling (to-be)
- Feasibility study → Systems proposal report

### 5.5 Systems Design

- Describes system specifications delivering functions identified during analysis
- Should address all managerial, organizational, and technological components
- Role of end users: user information requirements drive system building; insufficient user involvement is major cause of system failure

### 5.6 Programming and Testing

- **Programming**: system specifications translated into software program code
- **Testing**: Unit testing (each program separately) → System testing (system as a whole) → Acceptance testing (ready for production)
- **Test plan**: all preparations for series of tests

### 5.7 Conversion (切换)

Four main strategies:
- **Parallel strategy**: old and new run simultaneously
- **Direct cutover**: immediate switch
- **Pilot study**: test in one location first
- **Phased approach**: gradual rollout

Requires end-user training and finalization of detailed documentation.

### 5.8 Production and Maintenance

- System reviewed to determine if revisions needed; may include post-implementation audit
- Maintenance breakdown: ~20% debugging/emergency; ~20% hardware/software/data changes; ~60% user enhancements, improving documentation, recoding

### 5.9 Methodologies vs Approaches

**Methodologies** (methods for modeling and designing systems):
- Two major alternatives: (1) Structured methodologies; (2) Object-oriented development

**Approaches** (methods for organizing core activities):
- Five major alternatives: (1) Traditional systems life cycle; (2) Prototyping; (3) Application software packages; (4) End-user development; (5) Outsourcing

### 5.10 Structured Methodologies

- Structured: step-by-step, progressive
- Process-oriented: focusing on modeling processes or actions that manipulate data
- Separate data from processes
- Typical tools: ER diagram (ERD) — represent data structure; Data flow diagram (DFD) — represent processes and data flows; Structure chart — top-down chart

### 5.11 Object-Oriented Development

- **Object**: basic unit of analysis and design; combines data and processes that operate on those data; data accessed only by associated operations
- Based on concepts of **class** and **inheritance**
- More iterative and incremental; potentially reduces time and cost (objects are reusable)

### 5.12 Computer-Aided Software Engineering (CASE)

- Software tools to automate development and reduce repetitive work
- Includes: graphics facilities, screen/report generators, analysis/checking tools, data dictionaries, code/documentation generators
- Widely accepted standard: UML

### 5.13 Traditional Systems Life Cycle (生命周期法)

- Oldest method; phased approach with formal stages
- "Waterfall" approach: one stage finishes before next begins
- Formal division of labor between end users and IS specialists
- Emphasizes formal specifications and paperwork
- Still used for large complex systems; can be costly, time-consuming, inflexible

### 5.14 Prototyping

- Building experimental system rapidly and inexpensively for end users to evaluate
- **Advantages**: useful if uncertainty in requirements or design solutions; more likely to fulfill end-user requirements; often used for end-user interface design
- **Disadvantages**: may gloss over essential steps; may not accommodate large data/user volumes; may not undergo full testing or documentation

### 5.15 End-User Development

- Allows end users to develop simple IS with little or no help from technical specialists
- **Advantages**: more rapid completion; high level of user involvement and satisfaction
- **Disadvantages**: not designed for processing-intensive applications; inadequate management/control/testing/documentation; loss of control over data
- Could be increasingly effective with AI support

### 5.16 Application Software Packages and Cloud Software Services

- Save time and money; many offer customization features
- Evaluation criteria: functions provided, flexibility, user friendliness, vendor quality, required resources, organizational change

### 5.17 Outsourcing

- Forms: Cloud/SaaS providers; external vendors; domestic outsourcing (driven by need for additional skills); offshore outsourcing (driven by cost-savings)
- Risks: hidden costs (identifying/selecting vendor, cultural differences); opening proprietary business processes to third party

### 5.18 Managing Projects — Information Systems Plan

- Supports overall business plan; strategic systems incorporated into top-level planning
- Typical structure: Purpose of plan; Strategic business plan rationale; Current systems/situation; New developments; Management strategy; Implementation plan; Budget requirements

### 5.19 Portfolio Analysis

- Used to evaluate alternative system projects; inventories all IS projects and assets
- Balance risk and return from systems investments
- Information-intensive industries (e.g., finance): may have high-risk, high-benefit projects
- Non-information-intensive industries: focus on high-benefit, low-risk projects

### 5.20 Cost and Benefits Analysis

**Costs**: Hardware, Telecommunications, Software, Services, Personnel, Organizational disruptions

**Tangible benefits**: can be quantified (increased productivity, lower operational costs, reduced workforce); TPS usually produce more tangible benefits

**Intangible benefits**: not immediately quantifiable but may lead to long-run gains (more efficient customer service, enhanced decision making); MIS, DSS, ESS produce more intangible benefits

**Capital budgeting models**: Payback method, Accounting rate of return (ROI), Net present value (NPV), Internal rate of return (IRR)
- Limitations: focus on financial/technical, overlook social/organizational dimensions; costs not considered (organizational disruptions); benefits not considered (intangible benefits)

### 5.21 Dimensions of Project Risks

- **Project size**: costs, time, number of organizational units affected, organizational complexity
- **Project structure**: structured, defined requirements run lower risk
- **Experience with technology**: team familiar with hardware and software

### 5.22 Change Management

- Addresses the process of organizational change surrounding system building
- Very high failure rate (up to 70% for BPR projects)
- Challenges: employee concerns; resistance by key managers; changing job functions/career paths/recruitment practices
- **User-designer communication gap**: IS specialists have highly technical orientation; users prefer systems oriented toward solving business problems
- **Change agent (变革代理人)**: one role of systems analyst — redefines configurations, interactions, job activities, and power relationships

### 5.23 Controlling Risk Factors

- **Internal integration tools**: project leaders with technical and administrative experience; highly experienced team; frequent team meetings; securing external technical experience
- **Formal planning and control tools**: Gantt charts (visual timing/duration of tasks, human resource requirements); PERT charts (graphically depict tasks and interrelationships, indicate sequence)
- **External integration tools**: link implementation team to users at all levels; fill user-designer communication gap
- **Strategies to overcome user resistance**: user participation, education and training; management edicts and policies; incentives for cooperation; improvement of end-user interface; resolution of organizational problems prior to new system

## 06. Systems Analysis Fundamentals

### 6.1 Need for Systems Analysis and Design

Systems analysis and design: to analyze, design, and implement improvements through computerized IS, in support of users and the functioning of businesses.

Why?
- To align systems (solutions) with business targets
- To structure and guide systems development (a costly endeavor)
- To involve the users

### 6.2 Case: Lidl's €500 Million SAP Disaster (2018)

- **背景**: 德国折扣超市Lidl试图用标准SAP S/4 HANA ERP替换遗留库存管理系统，计划覆盖29国10,000家门店，2011年启动。
- **结果**: 7年花费€5亿后，2018年放弃SAP实施，回退到遗留系统。被广泛引用为"paving the cow path"的教科书案例——试图自动化而非重新设计破损流程。

| Phase | Critical Flaw | Consequence |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Analysis | 需求理解不足：Lidl业务逻辑用采购价计算库存，SAP标准用零售价。分析师未识别这一根本不匹配。 | 系统无法生成准确的财务报告和库存估值。 |
| Design | 拒绝业务流程重组：要求对SAP核心代码进行大规模定制而非适应最佳实践。 | 定制使系统变慢、不稳定、无法升级。 |

### 6.3 Case: Moutai's (贵州茅台) "Data Middle Platform" Collapse (2020)

- **背景**: 贵州茅台聘请阿里巴巴生态供应商建设"数据中台"，旨在统一分销网络数据、绕过传统经销商。
- **结果**: 项目成为业界知名灾难，据报茅台高管对供应商喊"滚出去"；平台基本废弃，退回人工Excel流程。

| Phase | Critical Flaw | Consequence |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Analysis | 忽视组织文化：未评估敏捷透明的"互联网式"平台与茅台僵化层级制SOE管理风格的冲突。 | 平台被内部利益相关者抵制，视为对既有权力结构的威胁。 |
| Design | 过度集中化、无回退方案：假设所有业务单元全数据集成，无模块化推出或部分采用的应急方案。 | 关键经销商拒绝提供数据后，整个平台变得无用。 |
| Stakeholder | 排除一线业务经理：仅从高层收集需求，实际运行日常操作的中层管理者未被咨询。 | 系统不支持实际工作流需求，导致变通方案和数据碎片化。 |

### 6.4 Case: MissFresh (每日优鲜) Supply-Chain Algorithm Bankruptcy (2022)

- **背景**: MissFresh开创"前置仓"模式做生鲜配送，依赖复杂的SCM预测算法将库存推到居民区微仓实现30分钟送达。
- **结果**: MissFresh崩溃，数千员工欠薪——说明"技术完美"的供应链系统若运营成本超过产品毛利则一文不值。

| Phase | Critical Flaw | Consequence |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Analysis | 低估需求波动性：假设生鲜需求可如包装商品般精确预测，忽视极端损耗风险和季节性波动。 | 算法持续过度备货易腐品，损耗率>30%。 |
| Design | 无利润意识成本建模：系统设计优化配送速度但未纳入超快履约的真实成本(冷链、末端人工、IT开销)。 | 履约成本是传统零售的3-6倍，破坏利润模型。 |
| Feasibility | 技术可行性优先于经济可行性：证明了算法技术上行得通，但从未验证业务是否负担得起所需IT和物流基础设施。 | 烧掉¥100亿，2022年7月申请破产。 |

### 6.5 Roles of the Systems Analyst

Three primary roles:
1. **Consultant (外部顾问)**: "I can tell you what to build."
2. **Supporting Expert (内部专家)**: "I'll help you build it right."
3. **Agent of Change (变革代理)**: "How can we make sure this gets done?"

### 6.6 User-Designer Communication Gap

| User Concerns | Designer Concerns |
|:---|:---|

### 6.7 Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

Phased approach to solving business problems through a specific cycle of analyst and user activities:

| Phase | Activity | Output |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Identifying Problems, Opportunities, Objectives | Interviewing user management; summarizing knowledge; estimating scope; documenting results | Feasibility report with problem definition and objective summaries |
| Determining Information Requirements | Interviewing; sampling hard data; questionnaires; observing decision maker behavior; prototyping | Understanding of how users work; complete info on people, goals, data, procedures |

### 6.9 Object-Oriented Systems Analysis and Design

- Alternate to structured SDLC approach for systems that must change rapidly in dynamic environments
- Uses Unified Modeling Language (UML) to model object-oriented systems
- Each object is a computer representation of some actual thing or event

### 6.10 CASE Tools

Recommended: Draw.io, Microsoft Visio, Lucidchart, Visual Paradigm, StarUML, Umbrello

### 6.11 Three Modeling Approaches

- **Context-level Data Flow Diagrams (DFD, 数据流图)**: Focus on data flowing into/out of system and processing; shows system scope (what's included vs external entities)
  - Process: some action or group of actions takes place
  - Entity: person, group, department, or system that receives or originates information
  - Data flow: information passed from or to a process
- **Entity-Relationship Model (ERD, 实体/关系图)**: Focus on entities and their relationships; static perspective
  - Three relationship types: One-to-one, One-to-many, Many-to-many
  - Steps: List entities → Choose key entities → Identify primary entity → Confirm through data gathering
- **Use Case Modeling (用例图)**: Part of UML; describes what system does without describing how; view of system requirements
  - Actor: a particular role of a user; similar to external entities
  - Use case: oval indicating the task
  - Four behavioral relationships: Communicates, Includes (<<include>>), Extends (<<extend>>), Generalizes

### 6.12 Use Case Scenario

Description of the use case with three main areas:
- Use case identifiers and initiators
- Steps performed
- Conditions, assumptions, and questions

### 6.13 Project Management Fundamentals

- Product oriented: building a website broken into parts
- Process-oriented: emphasizes importance of each phase

**Time Estimation Techniques**: Relying on experience; Using analogies; Three-point estimation; Identifying function points; COCOMO II

**Project Scheduling**:
- **Gantt Charts**: simple; good for end user communication; drawn to scale
- **PERT Diagrams**: useful when activities can be done in parallel; identifies critical path and slack time; easy identification of order of precedence; pseudo-activities preserve logic

## 07. Information Gathering

### 7.1 Interactive Methods to Elicit Human Information Requirements

Three main methods:
- **Interviewing**
- **Joint Application Design (JAD)**
- **Questionnaires**

### 7.2 Interviewing

Interviews reveal: interviewee opinions, feelings, goals, key HCI concerns

**Interview Preparation**:
1. Reading background material
2. Establishing interview objectives
3. Deciding whom to interview
4. Preparing the interviewee
5. Deciding on question types and structure

**Question Types**:
- **Open-ended**: "What's your opinion of the current state of ecommerce in your firm?"
- **Closed**: "How many times a week is the project repository updated?"
- **Bipolar**: "Do you agree or disagree that ecommerce lacks security?"
- **Probes**: "Why?" "Give an example..." "Tell me step by step what happens..."

**Arranging Questions**:
- **Pyramid Structure** (Closed → Open): useful if interviewees need to be warmed up
- **Funnel Structure** (Open → Closed): useful when interviewee feels emotional about topic
- **Diamond Structure** (Closed → Open → Closed): combines strengths of both, takes longer

**Closing the Interview**: Always ask "Is there anything else?"; summarize and provide feedback; ask whom to talk with next; set future appointments; thank and shake hands

### 7.3 Joint Application Design (JAD)

- Can replace a series of interviews with the user community
- Allows analyst to accomplish requirements analysis with users in a group setting
- **Benefits**: time saved vs traditional interviewing; rapid development; improved user ownership; creative idea production improved
- **Drawbacks**: requires large block of time for all participants; if preparation/follow-up incomplete, session may fail; organizational skills/culture may not be conducive

### 7.4 Questionnaires

Why Questionnaires: Scale (reach many stakeholders); Quantify (transform opinions into comparable data); Prioritize (rank features with statistical confidence); Segment (identify role-based needs)

Key Design Focus: Open-ended (qualitative insight); Closed/scale (measure attitudes); Ranking (force trade-off decisions); Avoid bias (careful sampling, neutral wording, balanced scales, random order); Ensure reliability & validity

### 7.5 Unobtrusive Methods (无干扰方法)

**What are they?**: Techniques for gathering information without direct interaction with stakeholders
- Examine existing artifacts (documents, emails, systems)
- Observe behaviors and environments (physical workspace, digital traces)
- Analyze textual data (feedback, communications)

**Why use them?**: Reveal actual vs. stated processes; identify hidden patterns; complement interviews/JAD with objective evidence; scale analysis across large datasets

### 7.6 Major Unobtrusive Topics

**Sampling**: Population vs Sample; Sampling error
- Simple Random: every item has equal chance
- Stratified: divide population into subgroups (strata)
- Systematic: select every k-th item
- Purposive: hand-pick informative cases

**Quantitative Document Analysis**: Extract numerical patterns from existing records — error rates, completion times, data volumes

**Qualitative Document Analysis**: Understand context, meaning, and narratives — emotional tone, recurring themes, power dynamics, communication patterns

**Text Analytics**: Extract themes, sentiments, and concepts from unstructured text data — concept frequency, sentiment analysis, co-occurrence, emerging themes

### 7.7 Observation — The Analyst's Playscript

Record decision-makers' actions in real-time using action verbs. Table columns: Decision Maker (who), Action (what they do — verb), Information-related Activity (what data they use/produce)

### 7.8 STROBE (Structured Observation of the Environment, 结构化环境观察)

Seven Observable Elements:
1. Workspace location & layout
2. Digital-tool placement
3. Stationary equipment (storage systems, filing habits)
4. Props & personal artifacts (sticky notes, printed lists)
5. External information sources (open browser tabs, reference materials)
6. Lighting & color (screen settings, interface themes)
7. Clothing & appearance

STROBE Symbols:
- ✓ NARRative confirmed
- X NARRative reversed
- ◇ Cue to look further
- □ NARRative supplemented

### 7.9 Information Requirement Analysis Output

Requirements-Specification Document (需求说明书) includes:
- Project overview (business context, problem statement, objectives)
- Stakeholder analysis (roles, goals, pain points, influence)
- Functional requirements (prioritized use-case list)
- Non-functional requirements (performance, usability, reliability, security)
- Business rules (domain-specific constraints and policies)
- Data requirements (key entities, attributes, relationship sketch)
- Assumptions & constraints (technical, business, resource limitations)

- **Autonomous Coding Agents/CLI Tools**: Claude Code (iterative multi-file tasks); OpenCode (autonomous development)

## 08. Process Modeling (I)

### 8.1 Prototyping (原型开发)

Four types:
| Type | Description |
|:---|:---|
| **Patched-up (拼凑型原型)** | Works but patched together; all features but inefficient; users can interact but retrieval/storage may be inefficient |
| **Nonoperational (非操作原型)** | Nonworking scale model to test certain aspects of design; used when coding is too expensive to prototype or when useful idea can be gained through I/O prototyping only |
| **First-of-a-series (首系列原型/示范原型)** | Creating a pilot; completely operational; useful when many installations planned; installed in one or two locations first |
| **Selected features (选择性功能原型)** | Some but not all features included; built in modules; part of actual system |

### 8.2 Agile Modeling Values and Principles

Values: Communication, Simplicity, Feedback, Courage

Principles: satisfy customer through working software; embrace change; deliver functioning software incrementally/frequently; customers and analysts work together daily; trust motivated individuals; face-to-face conversation; concentrate on getting software to work; continuous/regular/sustainable development; mindful design; self-organizing teams; rapid feedback; encourage quality; review and adjust behavior; adopt simplicity

### 8.3 Scrum: Typical Agile Framework

Iterative approach with time-boxed iterations called **Sprints (冲刺)**; cross-functional, self-organizing teams; founded on empirical process control: Transparency (透明), Inspection (检视), Adaptation (调适)

**Roles**: Product Owner (产品负责人), Scrum Master (Scrum主管), Developers

**Artifacts**: Product Backlog (产品待办列表), Sprint Backlog (冲刺待办列表), Increment (增量)

**Events**: Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum (15-min stand-up), Sprint Review (demo to stakeholders), Sprint Retrospective (process reflection)

### 8.4 DevOps (开发-运维)

A set of practices, cultural philosophies, and tools that automates and integrates processes between software development and IT teams.

### 8.5 Data Flow Diagrams (DFD)

Graphically characterize data processes and flows in a business system. Depict: System inputs, Processes, Outputs.

**Advantages**: Freedom from committing to technical implementation too early; Understanding interrelatedness of systems and subsystems; Communicating current system knowledge to users; Analysis of proposed system

**Basic Rules**:
- Must have at least one process
- No freestanding objects
- Process must have both input and output data flow
- Data store must be connected to at least one process
- External entities should not be connected to one another

### 8.6 Developing DFDs Using Top-Down Approach

1. Make list of business activities → determine External entities, Data flows, Processes, Data stores
2. **Context Diagram**: Highest level; contains only one process (number 0) representing entire system; all external entities and major data flows shown
3. **Diagram 0**: Explosion of context diagram; up to 9 processes; each numbered; major data stores and all external entities included
4. **Child Diagrams**: Each process may explode to lower level; child diagram number = parent process number; cannot produce/receive what parent doesn't; primitive processes = not exploded
5. Check diagrams for errors
6. Develop physical DFD from logical DFD
7. Partition physical DFD

### 8.7 Logical vs Physical DFDs

| Design Feature | Logical | Physical |
|:---|:---|:---|
| What the model depicts | How the business operates | How the system will be implemented |
| Processes represent | Business activities | Programs, program modules, manual procedures |
| Data stores represent | Collections of data regardless of storage | Physical files, databases, manual files |
| System controls | Business controls | Controls for validating input, obtaining records, completion, security |

### 8.8 From Logical to Physical

1. Derive logical DFD for current system from physical DFD (isolate unique business activities)
2. Create logical DFD for new system (add required inputs, outputs, processes)
3. Derive physical DFD from new logical DFD (determine user interfaces, process nature, necessary data stores)

### 8.9 CRUD

- **C**reate, **R**ead, **U**pdate, **D**elete — activities that must be present in a system for each master file
- **CRUD matrix**: shows where each operation occurs in a system

### 8.10 Event Modeling and DFDs

- Input flow from external entity = **trigger** (starts process activities)
- Events cause system to do something
- Create DFD fragment for each unique system event

**Event Response Table**: Event, Source, Trigger, Activity, Response, Destination
- Every row = a DFD fragment, used to create a single process on a DFD

### 8.11 Use Cases and DFDs

Each use case defines one activity and its trigger, input, output → allows analyst to work with users to understand processes and create single DFD fragment.

### 8.12 Partitioning

Dividing DFDs into collections of manual procedures and computer programs.

Purposes: Different user groups, Timing, Similar tasks, Efficiency, Consistency of data, Security → Partitions System Modules

## 09. Process Modeling (II)

### 9.1 Data Dictionary

A reference work of data about data (metadata). Collects and coordinates data terms, and confirms what each term means to different people in the organization.

**Data Dictionary Categories**: Data flows, Data structures, Elements, Data stores

### 9.2 Defining the Data Flow

Entries: ID, Unique descriptive name, General description, Source, Destination, Type of data flow, Name of data structure describing elements, Volume per unit time, Comments/notations area

### 9.3 Data Structures

Made up of smaller structures and elements. Algebraic notation:
- `=` means "is composed of"
- `+` means "and"
- `{}` means repetitive elements
- `[]` for either/or situation
- `()` for optional element

### 9.4 Data Elements

Entries: Element ID, Name, Aliases, Short description, Base or derived, Element length, Type of data, Input/output formats, Validation criteria, Default value, Comments

**Format Character Codes**: X (any character), 9 (numbers only), Z (leading zeros as spaces), `,` (insert commas), `.` (insert period), `/` (insert slashes), `-` (insert hyphen), V (decimal position)

### 9.5 Data Store Entries

Data store ID, Name, Alias, Short description, File type, File format, Max/average number of records + growth/year, File/data set name, Data structure name, Primary and secondary keys, Comments

### 9.6 Creating and Using the Data Dictionary

- Entries created after DFD completion or during development using top-down approach
- Uses: Create database tables, Create screens/reports/forms, Generate computer language source code, Analyze system design (detect flaws/clarifications)

### 9.7 Process Specifications (Minispecs)

Created for primitive processes and some higher-level processes on DFDs.

**Goals**: Reduce process ambiguity; Obtain precise description of what is accomplished; Validate system design

**Elements**: Process number/name, Description, Input/Output data flows, Type of process, Uses prewritten code, Process logic description, Logic method reference, Unresolved issues

### 9.8 Logic Description Methods

**(1) Structured English (结构化自然语言)**:
- Used when logic involves formulas/iteration, or structured decisions not complex
- Based on structured logic + simple English statements

| Type | Example |
|:---|:---|
| Sequential | Action #1; Action #2; Action #3 |
| Decision | IF Condition THEN Action A ELSE Action B ENDIF |
| Case | IF Case #1 THEN Action #1 ELSE IF Case #2... |
| Iteration | DO WHILE there are customers... ENDDO |

**Advantages**: Clarifies logic/relationships; Effective communication tool (teachable to users)

**(2) Decision Tables (决策表)**:
- Table of rows and columns, four quadrants: Conditions, Condition alternatives, Actions, Rules for executing actions
- **Advantages**: Help ensure completeness; Easy to check for impossible situations, contradictions, redundancy
- Four main problems to check: Incompleteness, Impossible situations, Contradictions, Redundancy

**(3) Decision Trees (决策树)**:
- Used when complex branching in structured decision process
- Useful when sequence of decisions must be kept in particular order
- Build left to right, listing all possible alternatives before moving right
- **Advantages**: order of checking conditions/actions immediately noticeable; more readily understood than decision tables

### 9.9 Selecting a Structured Decision Analysis Technique

- **Structured English**: many repetitious actions OR communication to end users is important
- **Decision tables**: complex combination of conditions, actions, rules OR need to avoid impossible situations/redundancies/contradictions
- **Decision trees**: sequence of conditions/actions is critical OR not every condition relevant to every action

## 10. Object-Oriented System Analysis and Design

### 10.1 Object-Oriented Concepts

**Objects (对象)**: Persons, places, or things relevant to system being analyzed (customers, items, orders). Consists of: Structure (attributes), Behavior (methods)

**Classes (类)**: Defines shared attributes and behaviors found in each object in the class
- **Attribute (属性)**: property possessed by all objects of the class
- **Method (方法/手段)**: action that can be requested from any object of the class
- **Instantiate (实例化)**: object created from a class

**Inheritance (继承)**: Derived class inherits all attributes and behaviors of base class; reduces programming labor; feature only found in OO systems

**Polymorphism (多态)**: Same method name, different behaviors depending on the object class

### 10.2 Unified Modeling Language (UML) Components

Three components:
- **Things**: Structural (classes, interfaces, use cases), Behavioral (interactions, state machines), Group (boundaries), Annotation (notes)
- **Relationships**: Structural (dependencies, aggregations, associations, generalizations); Behavioral (communicates, includes, extends, generalizes)
- **Diagrams**

### 10.3 UML Diagrams

**Structural Diagrams**: Class diagrams, Object diagrams, Component diagrams, Deployment diagrams

**Behavioral Diagrams**: Use case diagrams, Sequence diagrams, Collaboration/Communication diagrams, Statechart diagrams, Activity diagrams

### 10.4 UML Modeling Steps

1. **Use case diagram**: Describing what the system does — starting point
2. **Activity diagram**: Illustrates overall flow of activities
3. **Sequence diagrams + Communication diagrams**: Show sequence of activities and class relationships
4. **Class diagrams**: Show classes and relationships
5. **Statechart diagrams**: Show state transitions

### 10.5 Use Case Diagrams

Describes what system does without describing how. Based on interactions and relationships of individual use cases.

Four Behavioral Relationships:
| Relationship | Symbol | Meaning |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Communicates | Line (no arrowheads) | Actor connected to use case |
| Includes | <<include>> | Common behavior to more than one use case; arrow to common use case |
| Extends | <<extend>> | Handles exceptions from basic use case; arrow from extended to basic |
| Generalizes | Arrow to general thing | One thing more general than another; arrow to general thing |

### 10.6 Activity Diagrams

Show sequence of activities in a process, including sequential and parallel activities, and decisions.

Symbols: Rectangle with rounded ends (activity), Arrow (flow), Diamond (decision), Long flat rectangle (swimlane), Filled circle (start), Black circle with white border (end), Swimlanes (roles)

Use when: helps understand activities; flow of control is complex; need to model workflow; all scenarios for a use case need to be shown

### 10.7 Sequence Diagrams

- Illustrate succession of interactions between classes or object instances **over time**
- Show overall pattern of activities/interactions in a use case
- Emphasize **time ordering** of messages
- Three layers: Presentation layer (interface/boundary classes), Business layer (control classes), Persistence/Data access layer (entity classes)

### 10.8 Communication Diagrams

- Describe interactions of two or more things performing behavior more than any one thing can do alone
- Show same information as sequence diagram, but emphasize **organization of objects** rather than time ordering
- Made up of objects, communication links, and messages

### 10.9 Class Diagrams

- Show static features of system; do not represent particular processing
- Show nature of relationships between classes; show data storage requirements

**Types of Classes**:
- **Entity classes**: Represent real-world items (entities on ERD)
- **Interface/boundary classes**: Provide means for users to work with system (displays, windows, webpages, system interfaces)
- **Abstract classes**: Linked to concrete classes in generalization/specialization; cannot be directly instantiated
- **Control classes**: Used to control flow of activities

### 10.10 Relationships in Class Diagrams

**Associations**: Simplest type; reflexive association possible

**Whole/Part**:
- **Aggregation (聚合)**: whole composed of sum of parts
- **Collection (集合)**: members may change but whole retains identity
- **Composition (组成)**: whole has responsibility for parts

**Association classes (关联类)**: break up many-to-many associations between classes

### 10.11 Statechart Diagrams

- Examine different states an object may have; created for a single class
- Events: Signals/asynchronous messages, Synchronous, Temporal events

Create when:
- Class has complex life cycle
- Instance may update attributes in multiple ways through life cycle
- Class has operational life cycle
- Two classes depend on each other
- Object's current behavior depends on what happened previously

### 10.12 Packages

Containers for other UML things; show system partitioning; can be component packages or physical subsystems; use folder symbol; may have relationships

### 10.13 Using UML — Summary Steps

1. Define the use case model
2. Continue UML diagramming to model system during analysis phase
3. Develop class diagrams
4. Draw statechart diagrams
5. Begin systems design by refining UML diagrams
6. Document system design in detail

## 11. Designing Output and Input

### 11.1 Core Principle

Right information, right person, right time, right medium.

**MVC Pattern**: View (用户界面) → Controller (请求处理) → Model (数据处理) → View (更新)

### 11.2 Output Rules

1. **Choose the right output method (medium)**

| Medium | Advantage | Disadvantage | Best For |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Displays/Dashboards | Interactive, real-time, quiet | Requires user focus and screen space | Dynamic data manipulation |
| Audio/Podcasts | Hands-free, transient | Disruptive in shared spaces | Individual alerts, transient messages |
| Mobile/Tablets | Highly portable, gesture-driven | Small screen, risk of loss | On-the-go data consumption |
| Printers/Hard Copy | Highly reliable, handles large volumes | Static, environmentally unfriendly | Archival/regulatory compliance |

2. **Keep it simple and uncluttered**: Uncluttered canvas (single screen, no scrolling), Meaningful grouping, Prioritize summarization over raw data
3. **Neutralize output bias**: Sorting bias, Graphical bias, Truncation bias (Y-axis), Aggregation bias — analysts should avoid unnecessary bias and make users aware of possible biases
4. **Provide context alongside summarization**: "A number without context is just a number. Context turns data into information."

### 11.3 Input Rules

1. **Choose the right input method (medium)**

| Medium | Advantage | Disadvantage | Best For |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Keyboard/Typing | High speed text entry, universal | Typos, requires desk/focus | Long-form data entry, coding |
| Voice/Audio Input | Hands-free, natural, accessibility | Ambient noise, privacy concerns | Field work, driving, accessibility |
| Barcode/QR Scanning | Instant, accurate, eliminates manual errors | Requires pre-printed codes | Inventory, retail POS, warehouse |
| Touch/Gesture | Intuitive, no peripherals | Finger fatigue, low precision | Kiosks, mobile apps, casual browsing |

2. **Ensure accurate completion**: Smart navigation (hamburger menu, breadcrumb trail), Smart defaults (anticipate user input), Context-sensitive help, Input validation, Fat footer
3. **Follow natural flow**: top-down, left-right
4. **Use proper GUI control for data type**

### 11.4 The Best Data Entry is No Data Entry

Enter what only humans can know; let the computer figure out the rest. A base data item should be entered only once at only one point.

## 12. Designing Databases and Human-Computer Interaction

### 12.1 Normalization (规范化)

Three anomalies of unnormalized data: Update (phone only updated in one row), Insert (can't add customer without order), Delete (last order gone → customer gone)

**Normalization = eliminating multiple copies of one truth.**

| Form | Rule | Plain Explanation |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **1NF** | Atomic values, no repeating groups | One cell, one fact |
| **2NF** | 1NF + no partial dependency on composite key | Don't mix two things in one table |
| **3NF** | 2NF + no transitive dependency | Non-key attributes depend on the key only |

Practice: Aim for 3NF by default; Denormalize INTENTIONALLY when query performance demands it; Normalize for correctness, Denormalize for performance, Document both.

### 12.2 OLTP vs OLAP

| | OLTP (Operational) | OLAP (Analytical) |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Purpose | Run the business | Understand the business |
| Focus | Consistency | Analyzability |
| Workload | Many small transactions | Few large queries |
| Schema | Normalized (2NF - 3NF) | Denormalized |
| Example | Place an order | Which products grew 30% YoY in tier-3 cities? |

### 12.3 NoSQL Databases

| Type | Example | Sacrifices | Gains |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Key-Value | Redis, DynamoDB | Query expressiveness | Brutal speed |
| Document | MongoDB | Strict schema | Flexible nested data |
| Column-Family | Cassandra, HBase | Joins, transactions | Massive write scale |
| Graph | Neo4j | Tabular thinking | Native relationship traversal |

### 12.4 Vector Databases (向量数据库)

Core insight: Store MEANING as COORDINATES, not text. A neural network (embedding model) learns coordinates by reading the Internet.
- Traditional DB: find books by ISBN NUMBER
- Vector DB: find books by THEIR POSITION ON A MAP

Embedding model examples: OpenAI text-embedding-3-small (1536 dims); BGE, M3E, Sentence-BERT (Chinese-friendly); CLIP (text+image), Whisper (audio)

Tools: Pinecone (cloud), Weaviate/Milvus (OSS), ChromaDB (local), pgvector (in Postgres)

### 12.5 Databases Summary

- **Transaction Processing (OLTP)** → Relational databases, normalized (2NF-3NF)
- **Data warehousing, structured analytics (OLAP)** → Relational databases, denormalized
- **Fast and flexible data access** → NoSQL databases
- **Semantic search and AI retrieval** → Vector databases

### 12.6 HCI: Fit Between Human, Computer, and Task

Designing for HCI means ensuring system functionality and usability, providing effective user interaction support, and enhancing pleasant user experience.

**Fit**: between human, the computer, and the task

### 12.7 Types of User Interfaces

- Natural-language interfaces
- Question-and-answer interfaces
- Menus
- Form-fill interfaces
- Command-language interfaces (CLIs)
- Graphical User Interfaces (GUIs)
- Web interfaces

They are not mutually exclusive (e.g., VSCode = GUI + Menus + Command Language + Natural Language/Copilot).

### 12.8 Seven Types of Feedback

Absence of feedback = anxiety = retry = error.

1. Accepting input: "I heard you" (button press, field highlight)
2. Recognizing task: "I understand what you want"
3. Producing more information: "I need more from you"
4. Accepting/processing: "Working on it..." (spinner, progress bar)
5. Rejecting input: "I can't do that" (with WHY)
6. Communicating delay: "This is taking a while" (estimated time)
7. Completing task: "Done. Here's the result."

### 12.9 The AI Era: Interaction as Conversation

| Era | User must... | System provides... |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Traditional | Learn SQL, filters, syntax | Exact match on structured data |
| AI-Native | Speak natural language | Semantic match + generated answer |
| Agentic | State a GOAL, not a query | Plans and executes multiple steps autonomously |

**Agentic Interface**: User expresses GOAL; system PLANS and EXECUTES multiple steps, asking user only at critical checkpoints. User does not OPERATE the system — user DELEGATES to it.

Requirements: Tools the agent can call; Memory across many turns; Human-in-the-loop checkpoints

### 12.10 Historical Perspective

- **1970s-2000s**: Users learned the database's language (SQL, menus, forms)
- **2010s**: Mobile/touch made interaction more natural; data stayed structured
- **2020s**: Vector + LLM let the system understand the USER's language

## 13. Quality Assurance

### 13.1 Purposes of Codes

- **Surrogate key (代理键, 无业务意义编码)**: Simple sequence code
- **Meaningful codes (有业务意义的编码)**:
  - Block Sequence Code (分类序列编码)
  - Alphabetic derivation code (字母派生编码)
  - Significant-digit subset code (子集代号编码)

### 13.2 Human Purposes for Coding

Keeps track of something; Classifies information; Conceals information; Reveals information; Requests appropriate action

### 13.3 Coding Types and Trade-offs

**Simple Sequence Code** (e.g., 00000847 for customer_id):
- Gain: Stable forever; Privacy-safe; Deletable PII without breaking references
- Cost: Not human-readable; Exposes scale to competitors; Requires separate display name

**Block Sequence Code** (e.g., ORD-20260328-001847 for order_id):
- Gain: Naturally time-ordered; Queries by date range = PK range scan; Operators recognize timestamps; Trivial table partitioning
- Cost: Counter centralized within day; Reveals daily volume; Slightly longer; Cross-day operations need date index

**Significant-Digit Subset Code** (e.g., LM-X3-W-STD for SKU):
- Gain: Human-readable; Sortable by any segment; Bulk operations in one SQL line
- Cost: Segment meanings can go stale; Space limits per segment; Violates "Keep Codes Stable"

### 13.4 General Guidelines for Coding

Be concise; Keep codes stable; Ensure codes are unique; Allow codes to be sortable; Avoid confusing codes; Keep codes uniform; Allow modification; Make codes meaningful

### 13.5 Effective and Efficient Data Capture

**The Best Data Entry is No Data Entry.** A base data item should be entered only once at only one point.

| Question | What to Enter | What NOT to Enter |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Is it changeable? | YES → Must capture each time | NO → Look up from master data |
| Does it differentiate this record? | YES → Capture (e.g., SKU ordered) | NO → Derive (e.g., SKU's name, price) |
| Can the computer compute it? | NO → Capture | YES → Generate (timestamp, ID, total) |

### 13.6 Types of Input Validation

1. **Missing Data**: Is a required field left empty?
2. **Field Length**: Is the value the expected length?
3. **Class/Composition**: Is it the right character type?
4. **Range/Reasonableness**: Is the value within a plausible range?
5. **Reasonableness (blacklist)**: Is the value on a blacklist?
6. **Comparison with Stored Data**: Does the foreign key exist in master data?
7. **Self-Checking Digits**: Does the embedded check digit verify? (Luhn)
8. **Pattern Match (Regex)**: Does the value match a format pattern?

### 13.7 Validation: A Pipeline, Not a Checkpoint

Two Layers of Defense:
- **Client-Side Validation**: Instant feedback; catches honest mistakes; improves UX; can be bypassed
- **Server-Side Validation**: Never trust client data; enforces business rules; protects against malicious input; the only validation that truly matters

### 13.8 Total Quality Management (TQM)

Full organizational support of quality management; early commitment to quality from analyst and business users.

**"Test-First" Principle (测试先行原则)**:
- Unit testing: tests each program separately
- System testing: tests functioning of system as a whole
- Acceptance testing: makes sure system is ready for production

**Structured Walkthroughs (结构化复核)**: Use peer reviewers to monitor programming and development; point out problems; allow programmer/analyst to make changes

### 13.9 Taming Complexity: Modularity & Documentation

Why Modular? Human cognition (no one can hold 100K lines of code); Parallel work (teams work simultaneously); Change isolation (fix one module without touching rest)

**Top-Down Approach**: Start from whole → break into subsystems → break into modules → each module does ONE thing

**Designing for Maintenance**: Keep modules manageable size; Pay attention to critical interfaces; Minimize modules user must modify; Maintain hierarchical relationships

**Documentation**: "If It Isn't Written Down, It Doesn't Exist" — Procedure Manuals (classic); FOLKLORE Method; 2026 Extension: Docs-as-Code + AI-Assisted Documentation (LLMs auto-generate, AI summarizes, Chatbots as "living documentation")

### 13.10 Training

| Elements | Relevant Factors |
|:---|:---|
| Training Objectives | Depend on requirements of user's job |
| Training Methods | Depend on user's job, personality, background, experience; combine lecture, demonstration, hands-on, study |
| Training Sites | Depend on objectives, cost, availability |
| Training Materials | Depend on user's needs; operating manuals, cases, prototypes, online tutorials |

Training is not knowledge transfer — it's change management.

### 13.11 Conversion Strategies

Four strategies: Parallel strategy, Direct cutover, Pilot study, Phased approach

### 13.12 Security, Recovery, and Evaluation

- **Security**: Authentication and authorization, availability, confidentiality
- **Disaster Recovery**: Backup and redundancy; assume everything will fail eventually
- **Evaluation**: Define what "success" means before you build, or you'll never be able to prove it after.

## 14. Conclusion

### 14.1 Course Roadmap: From Principles to Design

- **Understanding IS**: IS foundations, conceptual dimensions, infrastructure, types of IS
- **Analyzing organizational problems**: Information gathering, process modeling, OO analysis
- **Designing future systems**: Output, input, databases, HCI, quality assurance, implementation

### 14.2 When "We Need a System"

When organizations say "We need a new system," they may actually mean: Information is fragmented; Processes are unclear; Departments cannot coordinate; Data are delayed/duplicated/inconsistent; People rely on informal workarounds; Managers cannot see operational reality; Customers/employees/partners experience service failure.

The essential value of an analyst is not to deliver a system, but to provide a solution. A system request is often a symptom; the analyst's task is to diagnose the deeper organizational problem.

### 14.3 The MIS Analyst's Mindset

- Information systems are socio-technical systems
- Technology alone does not solve organizational problems
- Good analysis begins with understanding current work
- Requirements emerge through investigation and negotiation
- Models help clarify complexity
- Design choices shape behavior, memory, and decision-making
- Implementation is organizational change

### 14.4 Trends and Open Issues

**(I) From Internal Systems to Ecosystem Coordination**
- Traditional MIS focused on systems inside organizations
- Increasingly: Digital platforms, APIs, Cloud services, Mobile applications
- Organizational boundaries become more fluid; coordination extends beyond the firm; platform governance central
- **Core message**: The future of MIS is not only internal system design, but ecosystem coordination.

**(II) From Data Management to Algorithmic Decision-Making**
- Moving from Recording transactions → Analyzing patterns → Predicting outcomes → Recommending actions → Automating decisions
- Open issues: Data quality, bias, explainability, accountability, overreliance, human oversight, responsible governance
- **Core message**: When systems make or shape decisions, MIS becomes increasingly a governance problem demanding value-driven mechanism design.

**(III) Human-AI Relationship**
- What should humans do? What should AI do? When should AI confirm? When should humans intervene? How should AI uncertainty be communicated? How should AI actions be audited?
- Human-AI Relationship: Automation (自动化 — AI performs instead of humans) → Augmentation (增强 — AI helps humans perform better) → Guardianship (监护 — Humans raise/cultivate AI agents and bear accountability)
- **Core message**: The central design challenge is no longer only interface design, but responsibility design.

### 14.5 Closing Thought

Information systems do not simply support organizations — they help constitute organizations. They define what can be seen, what can be measured, what can be coordinated, and what can be decided.

Therefore, to analyze and design information systems is to participate in the design of organizational intelligence.

We should: Be critical users of systems; Be thoughtful analysts of organizations; Be responsible designers of digital futures.
